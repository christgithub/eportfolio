Collaborative discussion 1 - Initial post: What has led to the rise of agent-based systems and the benefits that this approach can offer to organisations

Manifesting across different layers of society, several factors have led to the rise of agent-based systems.
The continual reduction in cost of computing capability has made it possible to introduce processing power into places and devices
where it would hitherto have been uneconomic, and perhaps even unimaginable (Wooldridge. 2009). This trend has allowed companies to
leverage an incommensurable amount of processing power to achieve their business goals and remain performant in their field,
competing in an ever-increasing pace of delivery most industries are faced with.

As a side effect of globalisation, the omni presence of connected devices has prompted for the support of autonomous
architectures in order to manage extremely high throughput of data flowing through huge data centers.
In turn those hosting facilities offer distributed services and platforms such as AWS or Google Cloud. As a result,
the colossal amount of data streamed and logged has constrained to leveraging learning agents to support operational processes
such as resource allocation, scalability, resiliency or security. Cloud Computing is one of the most intensively developed solutions for
large-scale distributed processing. Agent-based cloud computing is concerned with the design and development of software agents for bolstering
cloud service discovery, service negotiation, and service composition (Sim, 2012).

Service-based industries, more specifically healthcare, infrastructures are increasingly challenged by the amount of requests handling admin tasks,
out-patient triaging and providing treatment at pace. To alleviate these challenges faced by clinicians, deploying intelligent agents was a logical step to support this industry.

LLM agents will be able to not only suggest diagnosis but also schedule appointments while taking into account patient location,
medical urgency and physician availability. Possession of long-term memory enables agents to recall a patient’s medical history,
medications and preferences, which facilitates personalised care (Qiu et al., 2024).

Creating Agent Dialogues

Create an agent dialogue, using KQML and KIF, between two agents (named Alice and Bob).
Alice is an agent designed to procure stock and Bob is an agent that controls the stock levels for a warehouse.
This dialogue should see Alice asking Bob about the available stock of 50 inch televisions, and also querying the number of HDMI slots the televisions have.



            ;; Alice asks Bob about available stock of 50 inch televisions
(ask-one
 :sender Alice
 :receiver Bob
 :language KIF
 :ontology warehouse-stock
 :content
   (available-stock (television ?tv)
                    (screen-size ?tv 50-inch)
                    (quantity ?q)))

;; Bob replies with the stock quantity
(tell
 :sender Bob
 :receiver Alice
 :language KIF
 :ontology warehouse-stock
 :content
   (available-stock (television tv50)
                    (screen-size tv50 50-inch)
                    (quantity 25)))

;; Alice then asks how many HDMI slots those televisions have
(ask-one
 :sender Alice
 :receiver Bob
 :language KIF
 :ontology warehouse-stock
 :content
   (hdmi-slots (television tv50)
               (number-of-slots ?n)))

;; Bob replies with the HDMI slot information
(tell
 :sender Bob
 :receiver Alice
 :language KIF
 :ontology warehouse-stock
 :content
   (hdmi-slots (television tv50)
               (number-of-slots 3)))

Collaborative Discussion 3 - Initial post: Deep Learning

The rapid advancement of deep learning technologies, particularly generative models such as DALL-E and ChatGPT, has enabled the creation of highly realistic and novel content across multiple domains. While these technologies offer significant benefits in productivity and creativity, they also introduce substantial ethical challenges that must be critically examined.

A key concern relates to privacy, data protection, copyright infringement, and misinformation, as generative AI systems can produce convincing synthetic content, including deepfakes, which may undermine trust, truth, and democratic processes (Al-kfairy et al., 2024). These risks are further compounded by the potential for algorithmic bias and the reinforcement of societal inequalities, particularly when training data reflects existing disparities.

Moreover, ethical concerns associated with generative AI are often multi-dimensional and context-dependent, making them difficult to manage consistently across different domains (Huang et al., 2026). Although mitigation strategies exist, their implementation remains challenging, particularly in high-stakes areas such as healthcare and governance, where existing frameworks may lack the flexibility to adapt to evolving societal expectations.

Another significant issue is the lack of transparency and explainability in deep learning systems. As these technologies become increasingly embedded in decision-making processes, the inability to interpret how outputs are generated can reduce accountability and trust, highlighting the need to align AI systems with broader societal values (Goktas, 2024).

Finally, generative AI raises concerns regarding authorship and academic integrity, as it can produce human-like content that challenges traditional notions of originality and intellectual contribution (Zohny, McMillan and King, 2023). While these tools can enhance productivity and accessibility, over-reliance may limit critical thinking and compromise the authenticity of human creativity.

In conclusion, these issues demonstrate that generative AI technologies do present significant ethical challenges, requiring robust governance frameworks, interdisciplinary collaboration, and responsible implementation to ensure their benefits are realised without undermining societal trust.

References:
Al-kfairy, M., Mustafa, D., Kshetri, N., Insiew, M. and Alfandi, O. (2024) ‘Ethical challenges and solutions of generative AI: An interdisciplinary perspective’, Informatics, 11(3), p. 58.

Goktas, P. (2024) ‘Ethics, transparency, and explainability in generative AI decision-making systems: A comprehensive bibliometric study’, Journal of Decision Systems, pp. 1–29.

Collaborative Discussion 2 - Summary: Legal and Ethical views on ANN applications

The use of LLMs in policing and surveillance activity has shown how predictive models can amplify the marginalisation of certain communities. The data selection process used to train these algorithms needs to be regulated and scrutinised so that we can avoid societal inequality(Jain, 2018).

Large Language Models in healthcare can pose a beneficial means in assisting doctors, thus saving time leading to prompt diagnoses and early intervention for patients. Transitioning to digital imaging in the medical industry has leveraged the use of CNNs for patterns recognition and classifying images. Exciting gains have also been made in predictive biomarker analysis. ML models have been used to identify predictive signatures from peripheral blood samples and tumour biopsy material, including analyses of whole-genome profiles(Hunter, Hindocha and Lee, 2022)

However we cannot ignore the “robotic responses” conveyed from LLMs based systems. Although the accuracy of CNNs in the medical field is progressing at an amazing rate helping with prognosis, diagnosis and assisting in tailoring patients treatment the necessary bond between patients and their doctor remains paramount. In diagnosing patients, human doctors have a unique way of making behavioral observations and showing a sense of empathy with patients that no machine can.(Cordero, 2023)

Collaborative AI in an ideal world, would yield the dream team; LLMs can work on the time-consuming tedious tasks that we as humans prefer to pass on to others. We are able to work on projects that computers cannot and in turn productivity higher and conducive to the increase of work generated between humans and artificial intelligence.(Slack, no date)
